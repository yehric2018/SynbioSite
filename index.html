Sit Down with Synbio

Learn about synthetic biology, the new biotech frontier, through a panel event featuring industry experts

Free Admission, Open to the general public

October 5, 2018 7:15-9:15pm

Doors open at 6:45pm

Hogness Auditorium, HSB A-420, UW


About

With the biotech industry rapidly evolving, it can be difficult to keep track of all the new and exciting innovations.

Washington iGEM is excited to help you learn more about these rapid advances through Sit Down with Synbio, a synthetic biology panel event on October 5, 2018 at Hogness Auditorium at the University of Washington! Synthetic biology is the rapidly growing interdisciplinary branch of biology and engineering.It affects many aspects of our daily lives, yet there is a lot of uncertainty and unanswered questions about this field because it is so new.

This event will consist of a keynote speech by Dr. Scott McIsaac, a Principal Investigator at the anti-aging Bay Area startup Calico Life Sciences. The next 50 minutes will generate a conversation with experts and the general public through pre-submitted questions, and the event will wrap up with questions asked by the audience during the event.

Come on out to learn more about the biotechnology industry, whether you just want to learn more about biotech innovations like GMOs, are interested in a potential biology/bioengineering major, or want to hear experts’ perspectives. NO biology experience necessary! Sit Down with Synbio is sure to be a casual, fun way to understand more about the innovations that affect your daily lives and revolutionize the future.

If you have any questions about this event or Washington iGEM, feel free to contact us at uwigem@uw.edu

Panelists

Scott McIsaac, Ph.D.

Principal Investigator at Calico Life Sciences

Dr. McIsaac’s lab at Calico currently studies the structure, functional connectivity, and maintenance of organisms’ regulatory networks through the process of aging. Prior to this, Dr. McIsaac completed his doctorate degree at Princeton University in quantitative and computational biology and then went on to complete two postdoctoral fellowships where in one he developed his passion for high-throughput molecular biology.

Herbert Sauro, Ph.D.

Associate Professor of Bioengineering

Dr. Sauro’s research involves developing software that can simulate diseases such as cancer so that physicians may be able to test out potential therapies on detailed computer simulations before conducting clinical studies on patients. Recently, he was appointed to lead the Center for Reproducible Biomedical Modeling which was also recently granted $6.5 million from the NIH.

Jennifer Nemhauser, Ph.D.

Professor of Biology

Jennifer Nemhauser is a Professor of Biology at University of Washington and an HHMI Faculty Scholar. Throughout her career, Jennifer has been drawn to projects where novel quantitative approaches, new technology and multi-scale biological questions meet-up. As a graduate student, Jennifer got hooked on the big question of how cells figure out where they are during development. She discovered that a simple molecule called auxin plays a pivotal role in relaying this type of information in a highly context-dependent manner. Her postdoctoral work allowed Jennifer to use first-generation genomic tools to begin building an organismal, integrated view of plant hormone signaling and development. This adventure continues in her own lab, where she has teamed up with Eric Klavins (Professor, UW Electrical Engineering) to recreate auxin signaling in yeast—using synthetic biology paradigms to ground-truth the predictions generated by systems-level approaches. Alongside her research questions, Jennifer is investigating how social and institutional systems shape the way scientists frame, analyze and disseminate their findings.

Jim Roberts, MD Ph.D.

Founder & CSO of Lumen Bioscience

At Lumen Bioscience, Dr. Roberts and his team of scientists have discovered how to genetically modify Spirulina, a blue-green algae that is exceptionally rich in protein and consumed world-wide as a nutritional supplement. They can now use Spirulina as a platform to manufacture ultra-low cost biologics that target human and animal pathogens. Dr. Roberts was previously Director of Basic Sciences at the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center, and founder of numerous biotechnology companies. He also helped to found America’s Foundation for Chess, a national organization that promotes chess education for children.

Directions

Hogness Auditorium is room A-420 in the Health Sciences Building at the University of Washington. We realize that the size of the Health Sciences Building makes it seem like a maze, but here are some directions to hopefully find it effortlessly!

We recommend that you enter through the main entrance of UW Medical Center.

  1. Enter the main entrance of UW Medical Center.
  2. At the front desk, turn right and keep walking until the end of the hallway, where you will reach the Pacific Elevators.
  3. Take the stairs/Pacific Elevators to the 4th floor.
  4. On the 4th floor, turn left and walk through the Dental Clinics double door. Keep walking until you reach Hogness Auditorium; there are signs posted.

Arriving by bus or light rail:

  • Bus: Get off at NE Pacific St & Montlake Blvd NE
  • Light Rail: Get off at the “University of Washington Stop” UW Medical Center is a quick walk from the light rail station.

Arriving by car:

  • Paid parking is available at UW Medical Center at the Triangle Parking Garage
  • This underground parking garage is across N.E. Pacific Street from UW Medical Center.
  • Enter the garage from N.E. Pacific Place, one-half block west of Montlake Boulevard.
  • A pedestrian tunnel leads from the garage to the third (main) floor of the medical center.
  • The garage has 500 parking stalls - 67 of which are labeled for disability parking, and nine are wheelchair accessible.
  • Height restriction is 6 feet, 8 inches.
  • Pay as you leave.

iGEM

Washington iGEM is an interdisciplinary team of students who run a synthetic biology laboratory and conduct independent research. As the only undergraduate-lab at the University of Washington, Washington iGEM participates in the annual iGEM Competition, the premiere international synthetic biology project competition started at MIT in 2004.

Since 2007, our team has provided undergraduates with unique and exciting opportunities. Offering an alternative to traditional learning in the classroom, our iGEM team offers the opportunity to go through the design-build-execute cycle of a student-run research and engineering project. iGEM offers experiential, realistic research experience combined with focused learning, preparing students for real research positions in the rapidly advancing world of synthetic biology.

This year, our project involves creating engineered nanobodies (special antibodies found naturally in camelids) with applications in a wide range of fields, including detecting medical and environmental toxins and optimizing metabolic pathways.
